HOW I LANDED MY DREAM JOB BEFORE GRADUATION

Follow Marcus Rodriguez's journey from struggling first-year student to landing a full-time position at a Vancouver tech company while still completing his final term.

By Marcus Rodriguez • October 5, 2024 • 8 min read

When I started at BCIT's New Media program in September 2022, I was convinced I'd made a terrible mistake. Fast forward to today, and I'm writing this from my desk at Innovate Digital, a Vancouver-based tech company where I landed a full-time position before even completing my final term.

THE ROCKY START

Let me be honest—first year was brutal. I struggled with JavaScript, couldn't wrap my head around Git, and watched classmates who seemed to "get it" naturally while I spent hours debugging the simplest errors. My first group project was a disaster. I was the weak link, and everyone knew it.

By December, I was seriously considering dropping out. I'd applied to the program thinking I loved "tech," but the reality of actually building things was overwhelming. The imposter syndrome was real, and it felt like everyone else belonged there except me.

THE TURNING POINT

Everything changed when I started attending the peer study sessions organized through PathwayHub. I met Sarah, a second-year student who had struggled with the same concepts I was wrestling with. She didn't just help me understand the code—she showed me how to think like a developer.

More importantly, she introduced me to the concept of building a portfolio early. "Don't wait until you feel ready," she told me. "Start building things now, even if they're terrible. Future you will thank present you."

"Start building things now, even if they're terrible. Future you will thank present you."

BUILDING IN PUBLIC

I took Sarah's advice and started a simple personal project: a workout tracking app. Was it groundbreaking? Absolutely not. But it was mine, and working on something I actually cared about made learning feel different. I wasn't just completing assignments—I was solving problems that mattered to me.

I documented my progress on GitHub, wrote about my learning process on LinkedIn, and shared screenshots of my work. Some posts got zero engagement. Others sparked conversations with developers I never would have connected with otherwise.

Here's what I learned: people love watching others learn. My posts about debugging sessions and "stupid mistakes" got more engagement than any polished demo ever did. Vulnerability was my superpower.

THE NETWORKING GAME

Second year, I forced myself to attend every industry event I could find. BCIT's tech talks, Vancouver meetups, hackathons—even when I felt like I didn't belong there. At a JavaScript meetup in March, I met a senior developer from Innovate Digital. We talked about React optimization (I pretended to know more than I did), and he mentioned they were always looking for motivated juniors.

I didn't ask for a job. I asked if I could buy him coffee and learn about his career path. That coffee meeting turned into an invitation to tour their office. That office tour turned into an introduction to their hiring manager. That introduction turned into a conversation about internship opportunities.

But here's the key: I didn't just show up asking for opportunities. I came prepared. I'd researched the company, built a small project using their tech stack, and had specific questions about their development process. I showed them I was serious.

THE INTERVIEW PROCESS

The technical interview was terrifying. They gave me a coding challenge and 48 hours to complete it. I spent the first 12 hours convinced I was going to fail. Then I remembered something my instructor said: "They're not just evaluating your code—they're evaluating how you think."

I documented my entire process in my README. I explained my decisions, outlined the features I didn't have time to implement, and listed the tradeoffs I made. I treated it like a real project, not just a test.

During the follow-up interview, they barely talked about my code. Instead, they asked about my problem-solving process, how I handled being stuck, and what I'd do differently next time. All those hours I'd spent documenting my learning journey on GitHub? That was my portfolio.

WHAT ACTUALLY MATTERED

Looking back, here's what made the difference:

  • Building consistently: Not big projects, just consistent practice. My GitHub activity graph wasn't perfect, but it showed up regularly.
  • Documentation: I treated every project like someone else would need to understand it. This skill impressed employers more than my actual code.
  • Community involvement: Helping other students on Discord and at study sessions taught me more than any tutorial ever could.
  • Professional presence: I kept my LinkedIn updated, shared my learning journey, and engaged with the tech community online.
  • Real projects: Building things I cared about kept me motivated through the frustrating parts.

ADVICE FOR CURRENT STUDENTS

If you're struggling right now, here's what I wish someone had told me on day one:

Start building immediately. Don't wait until you "know enough." Pick something simple that interests you and start. A to-do app. A personal website. A simple game. Just start.

Document everything. Write README files. Post on LinkedIn. Share your failures and what you learned from them. Your future self (and future employers) will thank you.

Network before you need to. Go to events. Talk to people. Don't ask for jobs—ask for advice, coffee chats, and learning opportunities. The jobs will follow.

Use your peers. The PathwayHub community, study groups, Discord channels—these aren't just social spaces. They're where real learning happens. I learned more from explaining concepts to classmates than I did from most lectures.

Embrace the struggle. Everyone feels lost sometimes. The difference between those who succeed and those who don't isn't talent—it's persistence and willingness to ask for help.

WHERE I AM NOW

I start full-time at Innovate Digital in January, right after I complete my final term. I'm still learning, still making mistakes, and still occasionally feeling like I don't belong. The difference is, now I know that's normal.

The job isn't a dream come true because it's perfect—it's a dream come true because I earned it. Every late night debugging, every "stupid question" I asked, every project I built when I felt like giving up—it all led here.

To everyone reading this from their dorm room or the BCIT library, feeling overwhelmed and wondering if they'll ever figure this out: you will. It won't happen overnight, and it won't be easy, but it will happen.

Keep building. Keep learning. Keep showing up. Your dream job is waiting—you just have to build your way there.

WANT TO CONNECT?

Find me on the PathwayHub Discord or reach out on LinkedIn. Always happy to chat with fellow students about job hunting, portfolio building, or just surviving the program.

Marcus Rodriguez

Second-year New Media student specializing in front-end development. Incoming junior developer at Innovate Digital. Passionate about helping other students navigate their journey through tech education.
